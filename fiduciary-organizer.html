Important Information for Estates & Trusts

Click to read guidance on estates and trusts▼

Estate Classification & Taxation: An estate is the legal entity created upon a person's death. It includes all probate and nonprobate property and continues as a tax entity until the estate is completely settled and distributed. The estate's taxable income includes items not distributed to beneficiaries in the current year.

Beneficiary Reporting: Each beneficiary receives a Schedule K-1 (Form 1041) showing their share of income, deductions, and credits. The beneficiary's individual return must reflect their K-1 income. Ensure accurate beneficiary names, SSNs, and addresses are provided.

Distributable Net Income (DNI): DNI is the key to determining how much income is taxed to the estate versus the beneficiaries. Income distributed to beneficiaries is limited to DNI, and those distributions reduce the estate's taxable income. Properly track distributions versus amounts that must be retained and taxed to the estate.

Fiduciary Deductions: Expenses incurred in administering the estate (fiduciary fees, attorney fees, accounting, etc.) are deductible on Form 1041. These same expenses cannot be claimed as deductions on the decedent's final Form 1040. Choose carefully whether to deduct administration expenses on the estate return or on the estate's income tax return (Form 1041).

Section 645 Election (Qualified Revocable Trusts): If the decedent's will or revocable trust provides that the trust continues for two tax years following death, the trustee/executor may elect to treat the trust as an estate for income tax purposes under IRC Section 645. This election can benefit the fiduciary and beneficiaries by allowing the estate tax rates and potentially better treatment of deductions.

Carryover Basis vs. Step-Up: Property inherited by the estate generally receives a stepped-up basis equal to fair market value on the date of death. However, inherited IRAs and certain other property use carryover basis. Properly identify and report the basis of all property to minimize capital gains when property is later sold.

Charitable Deductions: If the estate makes charitable contributions during administration, these are deductible on Form 1041. Track all charitable contributions and provide documentation (receipts, legal commitments, etc.).

Estimated Tax Payments: Estates must make estimated tax payments (Form 1040-ES) if the estate's projected tax liability exceeds $1,000. These payments are made quarterly and reduce the estate's final tax liability.
